Seeing "her" in sport

Throughout history, women in sports have fought for equality. Specifically, women were not even allowed to watch the Olympics in the past. After finally being allowed to participate in sporting events, women were then required to undergo gender testing to ensure that they were not males trying to pull the wool over their eyes. It wasn't until 1992 that coverage of women's sports in the mainstream European and American sports media replaced coverage of dog and horse racing.

Multiple studies have indicated that, to this day, female athletes still experience significantly less coverage than male athletes, and that the media's attitudes and language practices differ between the two. Female athletes are often overlooked, discriminated against, belittled, or stereotyped in sports media coverage:

• The media often emphasizes the physical appearance of female athletes rather than their athletic accomplishments. During the 2016 Rio Olympics, many media conflated the appearance of female athletes with their athletic performance, emphasizing their looks over their skills or accomplishments.
• The media tends to emphasize the personal life roles of female athletes rather than their professional roles. During the 2018 PyeongChang Winter Olympics, some media described the athletes on the U.S. women's ice hockey team as "moms" and "wives" rather than highlighting their teamwork. During the 2018 U.S. Open, several media reported on women's singles champion Williams, but most of the news was about her issues with her coach and family.
• The media often compares female athletes to male athletes rather than focusing on themselves. During the 2019 Women's World Cup, many media emphasized the achievements of female athletes as being "close to the level of men" rather than highlighting their independent achievements and athletic prowess.

These are just a few examples of how female athletes are discriminated against and ignored by the sports media. This unfair attitude not only affects the image of female athletes, but also the development of women in sports.

In order to investigate whether there is a similar problem in the coverage of women in Chinese sports news media, we crawled and analyzed data from four sports news accounts on Sina Weibo - Migu Sports, Sina Sports, Tencent Sports, and PP Sports - and obtained a total of 7,430 tweets and their related data in 50 days from 2022.12.10 to 2023.1.30, and attempted to analyze the amount of media attention, the amount of interaction and communication, and the portrayal of female sports practitioners in terms of their textual image, and ultimately obtained this visualization, in order to raise the public's awareness of female athletes' deserved equal attention and public image in the Chinese sports media.

Together, let's see "her" on the field and in the media.







· The Invisible "Her"


According to a survey by Cambridge University, more than 40% of athletes in sports are women; at international competitions like the Olympics, the percentage of female athletes is even higher, and this is increasing year after year - 45% at the Rio 2016 Olympics and 48.8% at the Tokyo 2020 Olympics. Based on the percentage of female athletes, we might assume that they would ideally receive the same or slightly less media coverage than male athletes. But this is not the case.

· The Neglected "Her"


In addition to the lack of visibility in the media, female sports practitioners are also a "transparent" presence in public interactions and communications.

We crawled the number of likes, comments and retweets of each microblog while crawling the content of microblogs as an indicator of the interaction and dissemination effect of news reports at the public level. It can be seen that in the microblogs of Migu Sports and Sina Sports, the number of retweets, comments and likes of male-related reports are significantly higher than those of female ones, which to some extent indicates that the public's attention and willingness to disseminate female-related sports reports are lower than that of male ones, and that females are once again neglected in the process of information dissemination.

However, the data from Tencent Sports and PP Sports show a very different picture: in the microblog data we crawled, their stories about women were significantly more popular than those about men. It should be noted, however, that the number of tweets about women in these two media is 14 and 12, respectively, which is too small to be taken as generalized evidence to support the inference that women's stories are more effective than men's stories in these media. Moreover, there are many female relatives of male sports stars (e.g. Lionel Messi, Cristiano Ronaldo, etc.) in the two media, and the popularity and attractiveness of these male sports stars may also affect the dissemination of the stories about their female relatives, but this is obviously not the dissemination effect that we want to discuss about female sports practitioners in the media coverage.

Does the invisibility and neglect of women in media coverage and mass communication have an impact on the persona of female sport practitioners in the public view? The answer is yes.

A study from Wilmington College in 2018 revealed that many sports fans believe that women's sports are boring compared to men's sports. At the same time women's sports are rarely televised and covered in the mainstream media compared to men's sports. The research team posited the hypothesis: does the media convince sports fans that women's sports are less popular by reducing coverage? The team enlisted a group of volunteer sports fans and intervened to promote women's sports to the experimental group by adjusting the proportion of gender-specific sports information they received on a daily basis, while the control group remained unchanged by completing regular questionnaires. The results showed that the intervention had an effect on the experimental group, with the volunteers significantly reducing their prejudice against female athletes after 3 weeks compared to the control group.

The team noted that increased coverage of women's sports could give the impression that women's sports are important. This expanded gender coverage would lead to increased exposure of women's sports and increased public favorability of women's sports and female athletes. Beyond the general public, many of today's sports journalists deepen society's negative attitudes toward women's sports by reducing and distorting the focus on women's sports (compared to men's sports) through televised broadcasts and other forms of media communication. These sports journalists need to give more equal attention to women's sports in order to improve public attitudes toward women's sports.







· The Scrutinized "Her"


Aside from the fact that the amount of media coverage and dissemination is significantly weaker than that of male sports practitioners, the identity and image of women in sports coverage is also intriguing. We categorized the content of the crawled tweets:

1. Identity of the person being reported.We divided them into 3 categories: athletes, sports practitioners (e.g., coaches, sports organization staff, sports journalists, sports commentators, etc.), and people unrelated to sports.

2. Topics for news coverage.We categorized them into 10 categories: events, personal life, social media activities, PR interviews, relationships, business activities, anecdotes and affairs, external evaluations, media materials, and negative evaluations. In order to prevent ambiguity, we explain a few of the indicators: ① non-sports practitioners to participate in sports competitions (such as amateur soccer league, marathon and other public events, etc.) is recorded as "unrelated people - events". ② The posting, liking and commenting of the main characters on social media such as Ins, Twitter and Weibo are recorded as "social media activities". ③ Non-commercial interviews with athletes, team promotions, decisions and announcements by sports organizations are recorded as " PR interviews". ④ If two or more characters are featured in the story and the relationship or interaction between them is depicted, the story is labeled as a "relationship" ⑤ Coverage of the protagonist's participation in a TV show, commercial interviews, co-branded advertisements, own brand announcements, and other publicity with private gain is labeled as a " business activities". ⑥ Reporting on the inclusion of the protagonist in a list, praise or criticism from others, etc., is recorded as "external evaluation". ⑦ Media materials such as photographs, paintings, posters, documentaries, etc., produced by the media, individuals or independent studios, etc., for sports practitioners are recognized as "media materials".

After analyzing these two indicators separately, we find that when the target of the report is male, the proportion of athletes and sports practitioners reaches 94%, while when the target of the report is female, the proportion drops to 87.96%. Many women appear in the coverage not because their careers or lives are closely related to athletics, but simply because they are the wives, girlfriends, mothers or daughters of sports stars.

The vast majority of coverage of male athletes and practitioners focuses on the events themselves, followed by PR interviews, personal life, relationships, and social media activities. The amount of coverage focusing on the event itself is more than ten times that of PR interviews and personal life, suggesting that the most attention paid to male athletes and practitioners, both in the media and among audiences, is on competitive sport itself.

Interestingly, there seems to be a shift in media focus when it comes to coverage featuring women. Although the highest percentage of coverage is still the events. However, men's events was covered more than ten times as much as their personal lives and PR interviews, while women's events was covered only about four times as much as their personal lives. Topics such as business activities and PR interviews were also over-represented compared to the topics covered on men. At the same time, women received much less negative news coverage than men. The gender of the protagonist was male in 96.36% of the negative reports.

Such a phenomenon confirms the aforementioned view of the current academic mainstream: very often, the women in the sports media are not athletes or sports practitioners, but rather family members and friends of male athletes. When focusing on female athletes, the media show an extraordinary interest in covering topics such as their appearance, private life, and business activities, while neglecting to analyze their athletic level and achievements. In these respects, the Chinese and Western media display similar problems.

Beyond exploring female identities and coverage topics of sports media, we conducted NLP text sentiment analysis on crawled microblog content to explore whether there are significant sentiment differences in media coverage of sports news for men and women.

The calculated text sentiment value for each microblog is in the interval of 0 to 1. The closer to 0, the stronger the negative sentiment of the text; the closer to 1, the more positive the sentiment. A sentiment value of 0.5 represents a completely objective and neutral text. In order to facilitate statistics and visualization, we use 0.05 as the unit of division, divide 0 to 1 into 21 small intervals, and the length of each interval is also 0.05, and count the number of sentiment values of all the texts that fall into each interval, so as to analyze the trend of the media's sentiment towards different genders.

Once the statistics are complete, we use the resultant values as a bubble chart as shown below. The larger the bubble, the more microblogs fall within this sentiment range. Please note that since the number of posts related to male is 6 times more than that of female, the original scale of the graph does not give an ideal result. Therefore, we adjusted the scale of the two sets of bubble axes, standardized the axes and scale interval lengths, and reduced the size of male-related bubbles to 21.4% of the original size based on the bubble size of female-related reports to obtain the following results:


Distribution of sentiment values by gender  Unit: Pcs




It can be seen that there is little difference between media attitudes towards men and women in terms of overall sentiment trends, with the vast majority of microblog content displaying significant positive or negative sentiment.

However, when we adjust the size of the most positive sentiment bubbles for males and females to be roughly the same size, the size of the negative sentiment bubbles for males is significantly larger than that for females. Combined with the small percentage of women in Negative Evaluations in the previous figure, we can make the inference that women in sports should be more careful and mostly positive in their words and actions on the field and in their personal lives, and receive relatively little media criticism.

We also found that in all the sports news that used nicknames, the words that often appeared in the common nicknames for men were " King", "Emperor", "Big", etc., while the common nicknames for women were "Golden Flower", "Princess", and " Baby". This once again proves that even in terms of nicknames, the media and the public still focus on females in terms of their gender rather than their achievements.







· The Recognized "Her"


In our previous research and analysis, we focused on the lack of coverage, poor representation, and alienated image of female athletes and sport practitioners in the media. Is it true that the Chinese sports media only has obvious deficiencies and no strengths in women-related coverage? We found the answer.

In a study from the Department of Communication Studies at Carlos III University of Madrid, Spain, team members quantified gender differences in the content of four sports accounts on Twitter. Focusing on the sports played by athletes in media coverage, they found that the majority (74.14%) of coverage of Male athletes was focused on soccer, followed by basketball and motor racing, which received less than one-tenth of the coverage of soccer, and even less coverage in other sports. Women's coverage, on the other hand, is more evenly distributed, with soccer still getting the most coverage (20.88%), but tennis (17.67) and other niche sports events (13.65%) also getting a significant share. Although women's coverage is lower than men's in most programs in a horizontal comparison, in a vertical comparison, women's related coverage shows better diversity and balance than men's.

Drawing on this idea, we similarly categorized the crawled microblogs by sport category. We classified sports into 14 categories: soccer, basketball, tennis, water sports (swimming, diving, synchronized swimming, etc.), athletics, badminton, table tennis, gymnastics, volleyball, billiards, ice and snow sports (short-track speed skating, figure skating, curling, freestyle skiing, etc.), e-sports, other sports (rugby, field hockey, fencing, wrestling, etc.), and sports unrelated events. Based on this metric, we categorized the items and statistically analyzed each microblog to get the following chart (dark blue is male related, light blue is female related):

The results of the statistical analysis were essentially the same as the findings of the Spanish research team. In contrast to men's coverage, which is overwhelmingly focused on soccer and basketball, and the underrepresentation of athletes from relatively obscure sports, news coverage of women is more comprehensive, with more coverage of table tennis, soccer, ice sports, volleyball, tennis, and many other sports, and more balanced opportunities for women from different fields to be seen and heard.

Notably, the Spanish team's study found that more than 90% of the coverage of swimming in its data was female-related, which the team explained by the influence of Spanish female swimmer Mireia Belmonte, which has led to a significant increase in media and public interest in women's swimming far more than men's swimming. In our data, women in ice sports, volleyball and other sports also get more coverage than men. We can reasonably assume that this is due to the influence of Chinese female sports stars such as Eileen Gu, Mengtao Xu, Ting Zhu, etc., and the increasing attention of females in these sports.
